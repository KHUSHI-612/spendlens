# Go-To-Market Strategy - SpendLens

## Target User (Specific)

**Primary:** Solo founders and indie hackers paying for 3-4 AI 
tools simultaneously - typically Cursor or Windsurf for coding, 
Claude or ChatGPT for writing/thinking, and at least one API 
key they're billed for directly. They approved each tool 
individually over time and never stepped back to look at the 
total. Monthly AI spend is somewhere between $80-300 and 
climbing, but they couldn't tell you the exact number without 
checking their bank app.

**Why this person specifically:** They make the purchasing 
decision themselves, feel the cost personally (it comes out 
of their own pocket or runway), and are exactly the kind of 
person who shares useful tools publicly. When they find 
something that saves them $150/mo, they post about it.

**Who it's NOT for:** Teams with a finance department. 
Enterprises with procurement processes. Anyone who expenses 
tools and doesn't see the bill personally.

---

## The Trigger Moment

Solo founders don't audit tools out of habit. The specific 
moment that creates urgency is **budget review season** - 
Q2 and Q3 when they're looking at burn rate, extending runway, 
or preparing investor updates. The question "what am I actually 
spending on AI tools right now?" comes up naturally in this 
context and SpendLens needs to be the answer they find.

Secondary trigger: seeing someone else's audit result shared 
online. "Wait, they saved $200/mo? Let me check mine."

## Where They Hang Out

**Primary communities:**

- **Twitter/X** - this is where indie hackers and solo 
  founders actually live. Build-in-public culture means 
  people regularly share tools, expenses, and savings wins. 
  A screenshot of a SpendLens result showing "$240 in 
  potential monthly savings" gets reshared without asking.

- **Indie Hackers** - dedicated community for solo founders. 
  A transparent "I built this to solve my own problem" post 
  with real numbers performs extremely well. The audience 
  is exactly the target user.

- **r/SaaS and r/ExperiencedDevs** - where solo technical 
  founders discuss tooling. Comparison posts and "which tools 
  do you actually use" threads are common. SpendLens links 
  naturally into these conversations.

- **LinkedIn** - secondary channel. Engineering managers and 
  technical founders share tool discoveries here. The 
  professional framing ("I saved $X on AI tools") performs 
  well with this audience.

## First 100 Users - $0 Budget

**The core insight:** SpendLens's shareable result URL *is* 
the distribution mechanism. Every person who completes an 
audit gets a unique link showing their savings. When they 
share it - which solo founders with a build-in-public 
mindset naturally do - the savings number in the Open Graph 
preview is the hook. No ad spend needed.

**Week 1 - Founder's own audit:**
Run SpendLens on your own tool stack. Share the result 
publicly on Twitter with exact numbers: "Just audited my 
AI tool stack. Was spending $X/mo. SpendLens found $Y in 
savings I didn't know about. Tool is free: [link]"
This is the most credible first post - it's real, 
it's specific, and it's the founder eating their own 
cooking.

**Week 2 - Indie Hackers post:**
Write a genuine "building in public" post: what problem 
you noticed, how you built it in a week, what your own 
audit showed. Include the link. IH audiences reward 
transparency and penalize marketing speak.

**Week 3 - Reddit:**
Post in r/SaaS and r/indiegaming with a genuine story, 
not a product pitch. "I realized I was paying for 
overlapping AI tools and built a free auditor - here's 
what I found" with real numbers.

**Week 4 - Hacker News Show HN:**
"SpendLens - free AI tool spend auditor, no login required." 
The no-login part specifically matters on HN. The shareable 
URL mechanic makes it worth trying even for skeptical users.

**Flywheel:** Audit → shareable URL → shared on Twitter → 
new users see the savings number → run their own audit → 
share their result. Each shared link is free acquisition.
